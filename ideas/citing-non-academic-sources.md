To rigorously include and cite non-academic sources like blog posts, documentation, and white papers, you should formally adopt the **Multivocal Literature Review (MLR)** methodology. This approach recognizes the need for "multiple voices" and legitimizes the use of "grey literature" (GL) by treating these sources as valid data points rather than just background reading.

Here is the structured methodology for citing and utilizing grey literature in your academic work:

**1. Justify the Inclusion of Grey Literature**
You must explicitly justify why grey literature is necessary for your study. In software engineering, this is often justified because it is a practitioner-oriented field, meaning that excluding GL would cause you to miss the current "state-of-the-practice". You can use a specific checklist of criteria to decide if GL is needed, such as whether the subject is complex, if contextual information is critical, or if there is high practitioner interest that is not adequately covered in formal academic literature.

**2. Classify the "Shades of Grey"**
Grey literature is defined as content produced by government, academics, business, and industry that is not controlled by commercial publishers. When citing these sources, you should classify them based on "expertise" and "outlet control" into three tiers:

- **1st Tier (High outlet control / High credibility):** White papers, government reports, books, and theses.
- **2nd Tier (Moderate outlet control / Moderate credibility):** Q/A websites (like StackOverflow), wiki articles, presentations, videos, and news articles.
- **3rd Tier (Low outlet control / Low credibility):** Blog posts, tweets, and emails.

**3. Implement a Systematic Search Strategy**
Your methodology must document exactly how you discovered the grey literature so the review is repeatable. This involves:

- Using general web search engines, specialized databases, backlinks, or directly contacting individuals.
- Establishing specific **stopping criteria** for your search. Because GL searches can return millions of hits, you should stop when you reach _theoretical saturation_ (no new concepts emerge), an _effort bound_ (e.g., only reviewing the top 100 search engine hits), or _evidence exhaustion_.

**4. Conduct Rigorous Quality Assessment**
Unlike citing a peer-reviewed paper, you must formally assess the quality of the grey literature using a predefined checklist. Before including a GL source, grade it based on:

- **Authority of the producer:** Is the organization reputable? Does the author have recognized expertise?
- **Methodology & Objectivity:** Does the work have a stated aim? Is it balanced, or is there a vested interest (e.g., a tool vendor comparing their own tool)? Are the conclusions supported by data?
- **Impact & Novelty:** Does the source provide unique insights? What is its impact metric (e.g., backlinks, social media shares, views)?

**5. Extract and Synthesize Data with Traceability**
Because grey literature often lacks the standardized structure of formal academic papers, you must maintain strict **"chains of evidence" or traceability links**. When extracting data, provide direct links or comments indicating the exact position in the GL source where the information was found. When synthesizing this data, you can apply qualitative coding or **argumentation theory** to critically evaluate the practitioner's assertions, asking questions like whether the writer is a credible expert and if their assertion is based on actual evidence.
