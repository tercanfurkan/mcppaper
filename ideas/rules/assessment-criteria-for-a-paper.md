The document provides a structured assessment matrix used to evaluate academic papers, specifically simplified from a data science master’s thesis grading rubric. It evaluates the paper across **six core categories**, outlining the specific standards required to achieve a **Grade 1 (lowest/basic), Grade 3 (competent/average), or Grade 5 (excellent/challenging)** in each area.

Here is a full breakdown of the six assessment criteria:

**1. Thesis Objective and Research Questions**
This category evaluates how well the topic is selected, justified, and defined, as well as the formulation of the research questions.

- **Grade 1:** The research problem and objectives are unclear, unspecific, or lack proper motivation, though the general purpose of the thesis is somewhat stated.
- **Grade 3:** The topic and objectives are well-delimited, but the subject matter is not particularly challenging.
- **Grade 5:** The paper tackles a challenging research topic that is well-outlined, focuses on essential elements, and details the research setup comprehensively.

**2. Scholarly Framework and Use of Sources**
This section looks at the theoretical background, the definition of concepts, and how analytically the author uses research literature.

- **Grade 1:** Shows significant shortcomings in area knowledge, relying on too few, irrelevant, or low-quality sources, though some basic citations exist.
- **Grade 3:** Demonstrates familiarity with the topic and applies critical analysis to mainly relevant background literature.
- **Grade 5:** Displays an excellent, in-depth command of the topic using high-end scientific sources, resulting in critical maturity and deep understanding.

**3. Research Data and Method**
This evaluates the acquisition, description, and suitability of the data, as well as the mastery of the chosen methods and handling of ethical issues.

- **Grade 1:** There are major shortcomings in the choice of methods and data, but the author manages to use some relevant materials to get results.
- **Grade 3:** Methods and data are well-argued and suitable for the problem, with generally justified analyses.
- **Grade 5:** Demonstrates highly competent, well-argued use of data and methodology that is clearly rooted in scientific tradition or literature.

**4. Presentation of Thesis Results**
This criterion assesses the logic, clarity, and visual illustration (tables, figures) of the findings, and whether they successfully answer the research questions.

- **Grade 1:** Significant flaws in reporting, even though some results are presented.
- **Grade 3:** Findings are clear, logical, answer the research questions, and use visuals effectively.
- **Grade 5:** Flawlessly logical reporting where visuals actively support the analysis, alongside a discussion on the generalizability of the findings.

**5. Discussion and Conclusions**
Evaluates the paper's critical and ethical examination, reliability assessment, connection to prior research, and the inclusion of insightful conclusions and future research ideas.

- **Grade 1:** Conclusions are brief or missing, and the writer's own input or original thought is not visible.
- **Grade 3:** The writer's original input is evident, showing a clear understanding of the phenomena.
- **Grade 5:** Shows deep internalisation of the topic with sharp, to-the-point conclusions.

**6. Academic Text**
This final category focuses on scholarly writing, argumentation, structure, coherence, referencing conventions, language, and layout.

- **Grade 1:** The text lacks a scientific style, though basic structure (sections/paragraphs) exists and arguments can mostly be followed.
- **Grade 3:** Features exact language, well-defined terms, clear disposition, and a good level of polish.
- **Grade 5:** The writing is smooth, logical, and consistent throughout, featuring flawless referencing and a professional, consistent style.
