The provided seminar slides by Mikko Raatikainen from the University of Helsinki offer a comprehensive guide on research methodology and scientific writing, specifically aimed at helping students navigate common challenges in Master's theses.

Here is a full breakdown of the concepts covered in the slides:

**1. Research Methodology in Computer Science**
Having an established research methodology provides a structured playbook that helps both the researcher to carry out the work and the reader to evaluate it. The slides highlight several key aspects of structuring research:

- **Types of Outcomes:** Borrowing from Gregor's taxonomy, research can be categorized into types such as Analysis (Type I) or Theory creation/testing (Type II). However, Computer Science most frequently relies on **Type V: Innovation building or evaluation**. This involves constructing or improving a method, tool, or algorithm, and testing its utility.
- **Methodology Hierarchy:** Research should be framed using a hierarchy:
  - _Approach:_ The overarching class of research (e.g., descriptive or innovation building).
  - _Method:_ The specific research method applied (e.g., case study or design science).
  - _Technique:_ How data was collected and analyzed (e.g., interviews or log analysis).
- **The Importance of Evaluation:** In constructive (innovation) research, simply building a tool is not enough; explicit evaluation is required. This can be done through expert opinions, quantitative measures, or market tests, but you must define and justify your evaluation criteria explicitly.

**2. Scientific Writing and the IMRAD Structure**
Scientific writing is not like reading a detective story; the reader should know the main message ("the butler did it") right from the start. The slides heavily advocate for the **IMRAD structure** (Introduction, Method, Results, And Discussion), conceptualized as a "time glass": starting broad, narrowing to specific methods and results, and broadening again for the discussion.

- **Abstract:** A structured 150-500 word summary covering Context, Objective, Method, Results, and Conclusions.
- **Introduction:** Should introduce the background neutrally, identify the gap or problem with existing solutions, state the specific research questions, and highlight the paper's contributions.
- **Background/Previous Work:** An optional section to dive deeper into key concepts or prior solutions in present tense.
- **Method:** A past-tense description of materials and what was done, detailed enough to allow the reader to assess validity and repeatability.
- **Results:** A factual, concept-centered presentation of findings using tables and figures, without injecting personal opinions. In technical research, this is often split into a description of the solution and the evaluation setup/results.
- **Discussion:** Answers the research questions, generalizes findings, and compares them to **Related Work** (comparable solutions). Crucially, it must address **Validity** (correctness/truthlikeness) and **Reliability** (repeatability).
- **Conclusions:** A short (1-2 paragraph) summary focusing on principal results, takeaways, and future work.

**3. Specific Writing Tips and Best Practices**
The slides offer several granular tips to elevate academic writing:

- **Writing for the Reader:** Use a formal, clear, and precise tone. Avoid synonyms for key terms to prevent confusion, and avoid ambiguous pronouns (like "it," "they," or "this") which obscure meaning. Use "we" instead of "I" to avoid sounding arrogant, but avoid excessive use of the passive voice.
- **Tense:** Use present tense for existing knowledge (e.g., "Earth is flat") and past tense for your own research actions and results (e.g., "Accuracy was 99%").
- **Referencing:** Be "concept-centric" rather than "author-centric". Focus the sentence on the method or idea and put the citation at the end (e.g., "X method (Smith 2023)"), rather than focusing on the author (e.g., "Smith (2023) developed X method"), unless you are specifically highlighting a controversial opinion or disagreement.
- **Structuring Text:** Apply "Separation of Concerns" to make sections modular and self-contained. Every paragraph should have one main topic, starting with a topic sentence that is then elaborated upon.
- **Using AI/LLMs:** LLMs are great for ideation or proofreading but should _not_ be used to generate raw text, as they tend to produce verbose, overly complex sentences filled with superlatives, which violates scientific writing norms.
- **Formatting Details:** Use the serial comma for clarity. When introducing a key term for the first time, use _italics_ and define it immediately, but do not overuse italics elsewhere.
